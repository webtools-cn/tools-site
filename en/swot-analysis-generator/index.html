          ❓ FAQs

          What is SWOT analysis?▼

          SWOT analysis is a strategic planning framework developed by Albert Humphrey in the 1960s. It evaluates four dimensions: Strengths (internal positive factors), Weaknesses (internal negative factors), Opportunities (external favorable conditions), and Threats (external unfavorable conditions). It's one of the most widely used business tools for strategic planning, competitive analysis, and decision-making.

          SWOT vs TOWS — what's the difference?▼

          The SWOT matrix shows the four analysis dimensions. The TOWS strategy matrix goes further by deriving four actionable strategies: SO (Strengths+Opportunities — offensive growth), WO (Weaknesses+Opportunities — improvement), ST (Strengths+Threats — defensive), and WT (Weaknesses+Threats — retrenchment). Complete your SWOT first, then use TOWS to develop concrete action plans.

          How often should I do a SWOT analysis?▼

          Recommended frequency: Business-level → quarterly or semi-annually; Project-level → at initiation + after each milestone; Personal career → at least annually (e.g., during goal setting); Competitor analysis → with each major market change. SWOT analysis is time-sensitive — external environments and internal conditions change, so regular updates are essential for relevance.

          How to write a high-quality SWOT analysis?▼

          Quality tips: ① Be specific about strengths ("3 patented technologies" not "good tech"); ② Be honest about weaknesses (objective self-assessment); ③ Support opportunities with data ("market growing 15% annually"); ④ Be specific about threats ("Competitor X raised $50M" not "intense competition"); ⑤ 3-5 items per quadrant is ideal; ⑥ Team discussions yield more comprehensive results than individual analysis; ⑦ Update regularly to maintain relevance.

          Common SWOT analysis mistakes to avoid?▼

          Common mistakes: ① Listing too many items (more than 5 dilutes focus); ② Confusing external and internal factors ("weak competitors" is an opportunity, not a strength); ③ Analysis without action (SWOT must lead to strategies); ④ Being too pessimistic or optimistic (balance is key); ⑤ One-time analysis (markets change — iterate regularly); ⑥ Individual only (lacks diverse perspectives). Avoid these for more effective strategic planning.
