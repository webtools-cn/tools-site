📚 Color Theory Fundamentals

Color theory is a core knowledge system in the design field. It helps us understand the relationships between colors and how to use these relationships to create beautiful and effective visual designs. The color wheel, as a visualization tool for color theory, arranges colors in a circle according to their hue relationships, allowing us to intuitively see the connections between different colors.

Primary Colors and Color Models

In traditional color theory, red, yellow, and blue are known as the primary colors, forming the basis of all other colors. By mixing these primaries, we obtain secondary colors (orange, green, purple), and further mixing produces more intermediate colors. Modern digital design typically uses the RGB (red, green, blue) color model, while the print industry uses the CMYK (cyan, magenta, yellow, black) model.

HSV Color Space

This tool's color wheel is based on the HSV (Hue, Saturation, Value) color space. Hue represents the type of color, such as red, orange, yellow, etc.; Saturation represents the purity of the color — the higher, the more vivid; Value represents the lightness or darkness of the color. By adjusting these three parameters, any color can be precisely described.

6 Classic Color Harmony Schemes

Complementary: Two colors opposite each other on the wheel, creating strong contrast. Ideal for designs that need emphasis and visual impact, such as warning signs and promotional posters.

Triadic: Three evenly spaced colors on the wheel, rich yet balanced. Suitable for designs that need multiple colors without being too cluttered, such as children's products and creative brands.

Split-Complementary: One color plus the two colors adjacent to its complement. Offers contrast while being softer than pure complementary schemes, suitable for most web and app designs.

Analogous: Adjacent colors on the wheel, with natural, harmonious transitions. Ideal for creating a unified, comfortable visual atmosphere, such as natural landscapes and literary-style designs.

Monochromatic: A single hue with variations in lightness and saturation. Clean and unified, perfect for professional, minimalist designs like corporate websites and SaaS products.

Diadic: Two colors 60 degrees apart on the wheel. Moderate contrast, suitable for designs that need some hierarchy.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

What is a color wheel?

A color wheel is a visual representation of colors arranged in a circle, based on the primary colors red, yellow, and blue and their derivatives. It is the core tool of color theory, used to understand and create harmonious color schemes. Adjacent colors on the wheel form analogous relationships, while opposite colors form complementary relationships.

What are complementary colors?

Complementary colors are two colors that are opposite each other on the color wheel (180 degrees apart). When used together, they create strong visual contrast and impact. Common complementary pairs include red-green, blue-orange, and yellow-purple. Complementary color schemes are ideal for designs that need to draw attention and create emphasis.

What is a triadic color scheme?

A triadic color scheme involves three colors that are evenly spaced around the color wheel (120 degrees apart). This scheme is vibrant and balanced, offering rich color variety without being overwhelming. Common triadic combinations include the primary colors red-yellow-blue and the secondary colors orange-green-purple.

What are analogous colors?

Analogous colors are colors that are next to each other on the color wheel (typically within 30 degrees). This scheme creates a natural, harmonious color transition, evoking a sense of warmth and comfort. It is often used in nature photography, gradients, and designs that require a cohesive, unified look.

What is a monochromatic color scheme?

A monochromatic color scheme uses a single hue with variations in lightness and saturation. This approach is clean and unified, perfect for professional and minimalist designs. By adjusting the lightness and saturation of one color, you can create a rich sense of depth and hierarchy.

How to use color schemes in web design?

When applying a color scheme in web design, follow the 60-30-10 rule: the primary color should cover 60% (backgrounds, large areas), the secondary color 30% (buttons, cards), and the accent color 10% (highlights, icons). Also ensure sufficient contrast between text and background (WCAG recommends at least 4.5:1) for readability.

What color formats are supported?

This tool supports three common color formats: HEX (hexadecimal, e.g., #FF5733), RGB (e.g., rgb(255,87,51)), and HSL (e.g., hsl(11,100%,60%)). Click on any color to copy its value in the corresponding format to your clipboard.

Is my data uploaded?

No. This tool runs entirely in the browser. All color calculations and rendering are performed locally on your device, and no data is ever uploaded to any server. You can use it with complete confidence, without any privacy concerns.
