How to Use

The Color Blindness Simulator is a free tool that helps designers, developers, and product managers preview how images appear to users with various types of color vision deficiency. Here is a detailed guide:

Step 1: Upload an Image

Click the upload area at the top of the page or drag and drop an image directly. The tool supports JPEG, PNG, GIF, WebP, and BMP formats. For best performance, we recommend images not exceeding 4096×4096 pixels. Once uploaded, the image is processed entirely in your browser and never sent to any server, ensuring complete privacy.

Step 2: Select a Color Vision Type

After uploading, the tool will display the image and default to "Normal Vision" mode. Click any of the color vision type buttons below to see the corresponding simulation effect. The simulator supports 8 modes: Normal Vision, Protanopia, Protanomaly, Deuteranopia, Deuteranomaly, Tritanopia, Tritanomaly, and Achromatopsia. Each mode is based on scientific color vision simulation algorithms to accurately reproduce the visual experience of users with different color vision deficiencies.

Step 3: Compare and Download

In comparison mode, the original image is shown on the left and the simulated effect on the right. Click the "Toggle Comparison" button to adjust the display. When you find the desired view, click "Download Simulated Image" to save the processed image locally. The downloaded image is in PNG format, ready for use in design documents or presentations.

Use Cases

UI/UX Accessibility Testing: Designers can use this tool to test color schemes for accessibility. For example, red-green color coding in data visualization may be indistinguishable for users with protanopia or deuteranopia. By simulating these conditions, designers can adjust to more inclusive palettes such as blue-orange.

Frontend Development: Developers building charts, maps, and dashboards can verify that color-coded information remains accessible to colorblind users. Ensure that color is not the only cue—supplement with patterns, icons, or text labels.

Education and Awareness: Teachers and students can use the simulator to better understand the visual world of people with color vision deficiencies. Converting everyday images to colorblind perspectives fosters empathy and promotes inclusive design thinking.

Regulatory Compliance: Many regions have incorporated digital accessibility into legal requirements such as the WCAG standards. Using a color blindness simulator for self-assessment helps teams identify and fix color-related accessibility issues early, reducing compliance risks.

Knowledge Base

The Science Behind Color Vision Deficiency: The human retina contains three types of cone cells sensitive to short (blue), medium (green), and long (red) wavelengths. Color vision deficiency usually results from the absence or malfunction of one or more cone types. Protanopia and deuteranopia are the most common because their corresponding genes are located on the X chromosome, making males much more likely to be affected.

WCAG Contrast and Color Blindness: The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) require a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 between text and background. However, meeting contrast requirements alone is not sufficient—high-contrast red and green text may still be indistinguishable for colorblind users. WCAG therefore recommends using additional cues beyond color alone.

Colorblind-Friendly Palettes: For multi-series data visualization, consider these safe combinations: blue-orange (the safest), blue-red-green (with careful saturation choices), and purple-yellow-green. Avoid relying solely on red-green, green-brown, or blue-purple pairs. Combine colors with different line styles, fill patterns, or data labels to improve distinguishability.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is this tool useful for designers?

The simulator helps designers and developers preview how their work appears to users with color vision deficiencies. It enables optimization of color schemes, charts, and UI elements so that colorblind users can clearly distinguish important information, improving accessibility and inclusivity.

What is the difference between color blindness and color weakness?

Color blindness (anopia) means a complete inability to perceive a certain color—for example, someone with protanopia cannot see red at all. Color weakness (anomaly) means reduced perception of a color; the person can still distinguish it but with more difficulty than normal. Color weakness is generally milder than color blindness.

Is my data uploaded to a server?

No. This tool runs entirely in your browser. Image data is processed locally via Canvas and never uploaded to any server, fully protecting your privacy.

What image formats are supported?

JPEG, PNG, GIF, WebP, and BMP are supported. For best performance, we recommend images not exceeding 4096×4096 pixels.

How common is color blindness?

Globally, about 8% of males (roughly 1 in 12) and 0.5% of females have some form of color vision deficiency. Red-green color blindness is the most common, accounting for approximately 99% of all cases. This means 4–8 out of every 100 users may have a color vision deficiency.

How do I design colorblind-friendly interfaces?

1. Do not rely on color alone to convey information; use icons, text, or patterns alongside color. 2. Use high-contrast colors and avoid problematic pairs like red-green or green-brown. 3. Offer multiple color themes. 4. Use different line styles and fill patterns in charts. 5. Choose colorblind-friendly palettes such as blue-orange instead of red-green.
