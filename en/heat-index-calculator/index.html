What Can the Heat Index Calculator Do?

The Heat Index Calculator uses the US National Weather Service (NWS) standard algorithm to calculate the temperature the human body actually feels based on current air temperature and relative humidity. In hot and humid environments, the heat index can be significantly higher than the actual air temperature. Understanding the heat index helps prevent heat exhaustion and heat stroke.

Use Cases

Case 1: Outdoor Exercise Planning

Before running, cycling, or other outdoor activities, check the heat index to determine if it's safe. When the heat index exceeds 32°C, reduce exercise intensity; above 39°C, avoid outdoor activities entirely.

Case 2: Construction Site Safety

Construction sites and outdoor workplaces use the heat index to schedule work and rest periods, preventing heat-related illness among workers. At dangerous heat index levels, rest frequency must be increased.

Case 3: School Activity Planning

Schools adjust PE classes and outdoor activities based on the heat index. When the heat index is too high, activities are moved indoors to protect student health.

Case 4: Travel Planning

When traveling to tropical or hot summer destinations, check the heat index in advance to plan your itinerary and heat protection measures accordingly.

Technical Background

The Heat Index calculation is based on Steadman's (1979) human heat exchange model and the NWS Rothfusz (1990) regression equation. This equation accounts for the reduced evaporative cooling efficiency of the human body in hot, humid environments. When relative humidity increases from 40% to 80%, the heat index at 35°C can rise from approximately 36°C to about 50°C — a significant difference. The NWS specifies that the Heat Index should not be calculated when the temperature is below 27°C (80°F) or humidity is below 40%, as the difference between actual and perceived temperature is minimal in those conditions.

FAQ

What is Heat Index?
Heat Index is the temperature the human body actually feels when relative humidity is combined with air temperature. High humidity slows sweat evaporation, making it feel hotter than the actual temperature.
What formula is used?
The NWS Rothfusz regression equation is used, applicable when temperature is ≥27°C and humidity is ≥40%. It is the international standard for Heat Index calculation.
How are heat risk levels classified?
Caution (27-32°C), Extreme Caution (32-39°C), Danger (39-46°C), Extreme Danger (46°C+).
Why does high humidity make it feel hotter?
The body cools through sweat evaporation. High humidity slows evaporation, reducing cooling efficiency and raising perceived temperature.
