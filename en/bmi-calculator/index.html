What Is BMI?

Definition

BMI (Body Mass Index) is an internationally recognized metric used to assess body fatness and health risk based on height and weight. It provides a quick way to evaluate whether a person's weight is within a healthy range relative to their height. BMI was first proposed by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet in 1832 and has been widely adopted by the World Health Organization (WHO) and health institutions worldwide for nearly 200 years.

How to Calculate BMI

Metric formula: BMI = weight(kg) ÷ height²(m)

Imperial formula: BMI = weight(lb) ÷ height²(in) × 703

World Health Organization (WHO) BMI Categories

  • Underweight: BMI < 18.5 — may indicate malnutrition or other health risks
  • Normal weight: BMI 18.5 - 24.9 — lowest health risk range
  • Overweight: BMI 25.0 - 29.9 — weight exceeds recommended range; diet and exercise attention needed
  • Obese: BMI ≥ 30.0 — significantly increased risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and other conditions

How to Use

This BMI calculator is simple and intuitive to use. Here is a step-by-step guide:

Choose your unit system: First, select either "Metric" or "Imperial" based on your preference. Metric uses centimeters (cm) and kilograms (kg), the international standard units. Imperial uses feet/inches (ft/in) and pounds (lb), commonly used in the United States and a few other countries. The input labels and results will automatically adapt when you switch units.

Enter height and weight: Fill in your actual height (accurate to one decimal place) and your current weight. If you do not know your exact weight, use your most recent measurement from a physical exam or a home scale. All data is calculated locally in your browser and is never uploaded to any server, ensuring complete privacy.

Optional parameters: Entering your age and selecting your gender enables additional analysis including BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate), recommended daily calories, and body fat percentage estimates. These parameters are completely optional—you can calculate BMI without them, but the results will be more comprehensive with them.

Review your results: After clicking the "Calculate BMI" button, the result area displays your BMI value, classification, a visual gauge, ideal weight range, basal metabolic rate, recommended daily calories, and estimated body fat percentage. Each metric includes detailed interpretation to help you fully understand your body status.

Practical Applications

A BMI calculator is widely used in daily life. Here are some typical scenarios:

Personal health tracking: Using a BMI calculator regularly (e.g., once a month) to track your weight trends is an effective way to manage a healthy weight. We recommend recording your BMI values and dates in your phone notes or a health app to create a long-term tracking curve. When your BMI approaches or exceeds 25, adjust your diet and exercise plans promptly to prevent weight gain.

Fitness program planning: Before starting a fitness program, measure your BMI to understand your baseline body status. For underweight individuals, the fitness goal should focus on muscle gain with a high-protein diet. For overweight or obese individuals, fat loss should be the primary goal, starting with low-intensity aerobic exercise and gradually increasing intensity. Those with a normal BMI can focus on both muscle building and body shaping.

Pre-employment medical preparation: Some companies and government agencies have implicit requirements for physical fitness (although weight discrimination is prohibited by law, excessive weight can affect performance in certain physical assessments). Use the BMI calculator 1-2 weeks before your medical exam to assess your status, and adjust your BMI to the normal range through reasonable diet and exercise if necessary.

Extended Knowledge

Limitations of BMI: Although BMI is the most widely used weight assessment metric, it has significant limitations. BMI does not distinguish between muscle and fat mass, which means muscular athletes (such as bodybuilders and weightlifters) may be misclassified as overweight or obese. Similarly, BMI cannot reflect fat distribution—abdominal fat (visceral fat) is far more dangerous than subcutaneous fat, but BMI is insensitive to this difference.

BMR and TDEE: BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) is the minimum amount of energy your body needs to maintain vital functions at rest, accounting for approximately 60-75% of your total daily energy expenditure. TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) is your BMR plus calories burned through daily activities and exercise. This tool calculates BMR. To estimate your TDEE, multiply your BMR by an activity factor: sedentary ×1.2, lightly active ×1.375, moderately active ×1.55, very active ×1.725.

Related health indicators: In addition to BMI, you can assess healthy weight using these indicators: 1) Waist circumference: ≥90cm for men and ≥85cm for women indicates central obesity; 2) Waist-to-hip ratio: >0.9 for men and >0.85 for women is abnormal; 3) Body fat percentage: 10-20% for men and 20-30% for women is normal; 4) Visceral fat level: 1-9 is normal, above 10 is elevated. Using multiple indicators together is more reliable than relying on BMI alone.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is BMI?

BMI (Body Mass Index) is an international standard metric used to assess body fatness and health risk based on height and weight. The formula is: BMI = weight(kg) ÷ height²(m). A normal range is 18.5-24.9 according to WHO standards.

What is a healthy BMI range?

According to WHO standards: Underweight is BMI < 18.5, Normal weight is 18.5-24.9, Overweight is 25.0-29.9, and Obese is ≥30.0. Asian populations may use lower cutoffs (23 for overweight, 25 for obese) due to higher metabolic risk at lower BMI levels.

Is BMI accurate?

BMI is a useful screening tool for most adults, but it has limitations. It does not distinguish between muscle and fat mass, so athletes may be misclassified as overweight. It also does not reflect fat distribution—abdominal fat is more dangerous than subcutaneous fat. Pregnant women, children, and elderly people require different assessment methods.

How can I reach my ideal weight?

To reach your ideal weight: 1) Control calorie intake and focus on whole foods, vegetables, fruits, and lean protein; 2) Do at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity exercise per week; 3) Get 7-9 hours of quality sleep; 4) Reduce sedentary time and increase daily movement; 5) Consult a nutritionist or doctor for a personalized plan if needed.

What is the difference between BMI and body fat percentage?

BMI only uses height and weight, so it cannot distinguish between muscle and fat. Body fat percentage directly measures the proportion of fat in your body and is a more precise obesity indicator. For example, muscular athletes may have a high BMI but normal body fat percentage. Conversely, some people have a normal BMI but high body fat percentage (known as normal weight obesity or TOFI—Thin Outside, Fat Inside).

Does BMI vary by age, gender, or ethnicity?

Standard BMI applies to adults aged 18-65. For children and teens, age- and sex-specific BMI percentiles are used. For elderly people (65+), a slightly higher BMI range (23-28) may be protective. Different ethnic groups have different metabolic risks at the same BMI—Asian populations typically experience higher diabetes risk at lower BMI levels than European populations, which is why some Asian countries use lower BMI cutoffs.
