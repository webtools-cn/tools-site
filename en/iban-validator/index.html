✦ What is an IBAN?

IBAN (International Bank Account Number) is an ISO 13616 standard format for international bank accounts. It consists of up to 34 alphanumeric characters including country code (2 letters), check digits (2 digits), and domestic bank account number.

✦ IBAN Structure and Format

IBAN structure: 2-letter country code + 2 check digits + domestic bank account number (alphanumeric). Length varies by country (e.g., Germany 22, France 27, UK 22). IBANs are often grouped but spaces must be removed for validation.

✦ Importance of IBAN Validation

Proper IBAN validation prevents transfer errors. The MOD-97 algorithm detects common input mistakes (single-character errors, transpositions, missing characters), ensuring international payment accuracy.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is an IBAN?

IBAN (International Bank Account Number) is an internationally agreed standard (ISO 13616) for identifying bank accounts. It consists of up to 34 alphanumeric characters, including country code, check digits, and domestic bank account number. IBAN simplifies international money transfers.

Is my IBAN data safe with this validator?

Absolutely. Everything runs locally in your browser. Your IBAN data never leaves your device. All validation (format check, MOD-97 checksum) is performed locally. The tool works even offline.

What is the MOD-97 checksum?

MOD-97 is the core IBAN validation algorithm. Letters are converted to numbers, then the total is divided by 97. If the remainder is 1, the IBAN check digits are valid. This ISO 7064 standard algorithm detects common input errors like single-character mistakes and transpositions.

What's the difference between IBAN and SWIFT/BIC?

IBAN identifies your specific bank account, while SWIFT/BIC identifies the bank itself. International transfers typically need both: IBAN for the target account and SWIFT/BIC for the target bank.

Do all countries use IBAN?

No. European countries widely use IBAN, as do parts of the Middle East and Asia. The US, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, and China primarily use domestic account systems. However, their banks can usually process IBAN payments.
