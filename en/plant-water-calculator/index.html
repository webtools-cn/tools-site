Watering Tips

Plant Watering Guide

Proper watering is the most important aspect of plant care. Both overwatering and underwatering can harm plants. This calculator helps you determine the right amount and frequency based on your specific plant and conditions.

General Watering Rules

  • Water deeply but infrequently rather than shallow and often
  • Always check soil moisture before watering
  • Ensure pots have drainage holes
  • Water in the morning for best absorption
  • Adjust watering based on humidity and temperature

FAQ

How to tell if a plant needs water?

The simplest method is the "knuckle test": insert your finger 2-3cm into the soil. If it feels dry, it is time to water. Other signs: drooping leaves, yellowing tips (overwatering), soil pulling away from pot edges. Different plants have very different water needs - this tool helps you calculate precisely.

Is overwatering or underwatering more dangerous?

Overwatering is usually more dangerous. It causes root rot from lack of oxygen, which is hard to reverse once it starts. Underwatered plants can usually recover with proper watering. The rule of thumb is "better dry than wet" - wait until the soil surface is dry before watering.

How does watering differ by season?

Spring and summer are growing seasons with higher water needs and frequency. Fall and winter are dormant periods with reduced water needs. In winter with indoor heating, air is dry and you may need to water more or mist plants.

Can I use tap water directly for plants?

Tap water contains chlorine and minerals that may harm sensitive plants. Tips: 1) Let tap water sit for 24 hours to let chlorine evaporate; 2) Collect rainwater (best option); 3) Use filtered water. Succulents and tropical plants are more sensitive to water quality.
